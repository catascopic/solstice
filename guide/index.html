2020 Maryland Solstice: Your Guide to Entry

You’re trapped in the year 1918. A pandemic rages across America. Your only escape is to reach the time machine and travel to 2020, where the world is gripped by a different pandemic, but at least we have Zoom.

But before you can get to the time machine, you have to coordinate with your fellow time travelers. Fortunately, you know Morse code.

What’s that, you don’t?

That’s okay, I’m sure you’ll pick it up quickly.

You’ve also been assigned a secret, unique codebook. The other time travelers also have one, but each one is different. These codebooks use a call-and-response format: if someone invokes one of the calls from your codebook, you must answer with the proper response to prove your identity.

Your codebook will look something like this (though it will have 32 total entries):

breakfast: nuclear
party: ancient
tomorrow: impossible

For example, if anyone signals BREAKFAST in Morse code, you must respond with NUCLEAR. You also must identify who you’re addressing; everyone has their own three-letter callsign. A sample chat might look like this:

ABC BREAKFAST
XYZ OCEAN

So if you were QRS, you would respond like this:

QRS ABC NUCLEAR

And that lets ABC know that the response to BREAKFAST is NUCLEAR, which gets us all one step closer to returning to the present day. Notice that XYZ needs the response to OCEAN. That word doesn’t appear in your codebook, though, meaning it was meant for someone else and you don’t need to worry about it. But you’ll have to check your codebook whenever anyone sends a prompt, to see if you’re responsible for the answer!

Meanwhile, you have a sequence of prompts that you need the responses to, but of course, these prompts are for the other travelers, who will need to consult their codebooks. You’ll need to transmit them your prompt in Morse code, and wait for someone to give you the correct response. In the above example, if you were ABC, you’d be waiting for QRS to give you the proper response to BREAKFAST. Once you had it, you’d type it into the text field and press enter.

Once the team as a whole has completed enough call-and-response exchanges, indicated by the lock icons, the video chat, uh, I mean the time machine, will become available.

If you get totally stuck, you can call the emergency time traveler hotline: 443-545-6177.

But wait, we’re doing all of this in Morse code?

Prettty much, yeah. Remember, communicating in Morse code builds character! You’ll be sending messages by tapping Space. So to send an S, three quick taps on your spacebar; for O, three longer taps. (Remember to pause between letters!) Fortunately, you’re a good enough telegraph operator that you can read Morse code naturally, so any messages you receive will appear in plain English.

Also, there will be a built-in Morse code chart, in case you happen to forget one of the letters. You can press Tab to view the chart, as well as your codebook.

Real telegraphers use pauses of varying lengths to signal the boundaries between letters, words, and sentences. But as fake telegraphers, we can use Shift+Space for a space, and Enter to end the current line.

And finally (now I’m being way too nice) you can delete your last character with Backspace. But only your last letter! Once you start your next letter, you’ll be unable to delete your previous one.

Your Callsign

Before you begin, you’ll have to pick a callsign. It must be exactly 3 letters, so it could be your initials, or your first name (if you’re lucky), or anything else that’s meaningful to you.
